# Natural Science

Notes on the fundamental branches of natural science — the systematic study of the natural world through observation, experimentation, and mathematical reasoning.

---

## Overview

Natural science can be broadly divided into **Physics**, **Chemistry**, **Biology**, **Earth Science**, and **Astronomy**. This page focuses primarily on **Physics** and **Chemistry**, which form the quantitative backbone of engineering and technology.

---

## Physics

Physics studies matter, energy, and their interactions. Its major branches include:

| Branch | Focus Area | Key Applications |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Mechanics** | Motion, forces, energy | Structural engineering, robotics |
| **Electromagnetism** | Electric & magnetic fields | Electronics, wireless communication |
| **Optics** | Light and its behavior | Cameras, fiber optics, displays |
| **Acoustics** | Sound waves and vibration | Audio engineering, ultrasound imaging |
| **Thermodynamics** | Heat, work, energy transfer | Engines, refrigeration, climate science |
| **Quantum Mechanics** | Subatomic particle behavior | Semiconductors, quantum computing |

### Mechanics: Classical vs. Quantum

Mechanics is historically divided into two paradigms:

- **Classical Mechanics** (Newtonian): Governs macroscopic objects at everyday speeds. Core concepts include Newton's three laws, conservation of energy, and conservation of momentum. It remains the foundation for most engineering disciplines.

- **Quantum Mechanics**: Governs particles at atomic and subatomic scales. Key principles include:
  - **Wave-particle duality** — particles like electrons exhibit both wave and particle behavior.
  - **Heisenberg's uncertainty principle** — you cannot simultaneously know a particle's exact position and momentum.
  - **Superposition** — a quantum system can exist in multiple states at once until measured.
  - **Entanglement** — two particles can be correlated such that measuring one instantly determines the state of the other, regardless of distance.

> The transition from classical to quantum descriptions becomes necessary when dealing with very small scales (nanometers) or very low temperatures (near absolute zero).

### Electromagnetism

Electromagnetism unifies electricity and magnetism into a single framework described by **Maxwell's equations**. It explains how electric charges produce fields, how changing magnetic fields induce electric currents (Faraday's law), and how electromagnetic waves (including light) propagate through space.

Key milestones:
1. **Coulomb's Law** — force between electric charges
2. **Ampère's Law** — magnetic field around a current-carrying wire
3. **Faraday's Law** — electromagnetic induction
4. **Maxwell's Equations** — unified field theory predicting EM waves

### Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics governs energy transfer and transformation. Its four laws define the behavior of macroscopic systems:

| Law | Statement |
|-----|-----------|
| **Zeroth Law** | If A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in equilibrium with C. |
| **First Law** | Energy is conserved: $\Delta U = Q - W$ |
| **Second Law** | Entropy of an isolated system never decreases. Heat flows from hot to cold spontaneously. |
| **Third Law** | As temperature approaches absolute zero, entropy approaches a minimum. |

### Optics

Optics studies the behavior of light, including reflection, refraction, diffraction, and interference. Modern optics extends into:

- **Geometric optics** — ray-based analysis (lenses, mirrors)
- **Wave optics** — interference and diffraction patterns
- **Quantum optics** — photon-level phenomena (lasers, single-photon detectors)

### Acoustics

Acoustics deals with sound — mechanical waves propagating through media (air, water, solids). Important concepts:

- **Frequency & wavelength** — determine pitch
- **Amplitude** — determines loudness
- **Resonance** — amplification at natural frequencies
- **Doppler effect** — frequency shift due to relative motion between source and observer

## Chemistry

Chemistry studies the composition, structure, properties, and transformations of matter. Its core branches include:

| Branch | Focus Area |
|--------|-----------|
| **Organic Chemistry** | Carbon-based compounds (pharmaceuticals, polymers, fuels) |
| **Inorganic Chemistry** | Non-carbon compounds (metals, minerals, catalysts) |
| **Physical Chemistry** | Energy and quantum aspects of chemical systems |
| **Analytical Chemistry** | Identification and quantification of substances |
| **Biochemistry** | Chemical processes in living organisms |

### The Periodic Table

The periodic table organizes all known elements by atomic number and electron configuration. Key groupings:

- **Alkali metals** (Group 1) — highly reactive, e.g., Na, K
- **Noble gases** (Group 18) — chemically inert, e.g., He, Ne, Ar
- **Transition metals** (Groups 3–12) — versatile, often used as catalysts, e.g., Fe, Cu, Pt
- **Halogens** (Group 17) — reactive nonmetals, e.g., F, Cl, Br

### Chemical Bonding

Atoms combine through several bonding mechanisms:

- **Ionic bonds** — electron transfer between atoms (e.g., NaCl)
- **Covalent bonds** — electron sharing (e.g., $H_2O$, $CO_2$)
- **Metallic bonds** — electron sea model in metals
- **Hydrogen bonds** — weak but crucial in biology (DNA, protein folding)

## Intersection with Technology

Natural science is the foundation of modern technology. A few important crossovers:

| Science Concept | Technology Application |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Quantum mechanics | Transistors, semiconductors, quantum computing |
| Electromagnetism | Wi-Fi, 5G, radar, MRI |
| Thermodynamics | CPU thermal design, power plants |
| Optics | Fiber-optic communication, LiDAR |
| Organic chemistry | Plastics, OLED displays, drug design |
| Acoustics | Noise cancellation, sonar, speech recognition |
